# Stanford CME295 Transformers & LLMs | Autumn 2025 | Lecture 2 - Transformer-Based Models & Tricks

## TL;DR

Lecture 2 of Stanford CME295, in two halves: Afshine covers the components of the original 2017 Transformer that have since evolved, Shervine the taxonomy of modern transformer models plus a BERT deep dive. After recapping self-attention (Q/K/V, softmax(QKᵀ/√dk)V, multi-head), the first half traces positional encoding from learned/sinusoidal absolute embeddings through relative-bias methods (T5 buckets, ALiBi) to RoPE — rotating Q/K vectors by position-dependent angles so attention depends on relative distance — which is what most current models use. Normalization moved from post-norm LayerNorm to pre-norm RMSNorm (fewer parameters, comparable convergence), and attention efficiency comes from sliding-window/local attention interleaved with global layers (Mistral) and KV-cache-motivated head sharing via MQA/GQA (GQA is the modern default). The second half classifies models as encoder-decoder (T5 family, span-corruption training), encoder-only (BERT), and decoder-only (today's LLMs — next-token prediction scales best). The BERT deep dive covers bidirectionality vs. causal masking, CLS/SEP tokens, WordPiece, MLM (~15% masking, 80/10/10 split) and NSP objectives, fine-tuning via a head on CLS, then successors: DistilBERT (distillation via KL divergence) and RoBERTa (drops NSP, dynamic masking, more data). Worth rewatching: the RoPE intuition, the MQA/GQA-vs-KV-cache tradeoff, and the BERT recipe — the load-bearing design choices that carry into modern LLMs and embeddings.
